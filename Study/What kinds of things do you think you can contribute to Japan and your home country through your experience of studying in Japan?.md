One of our scholars says about the unpredictability of the future, “I don’t know what job a child born today will have in the future.” It is very difficult to give a clear and precise answer to this question. Because even my research topic has not been precisely defined yet. And I am also not fully familiar with Japan, and even if all these things were specified, the scientific path is usually not predictable at all. However, I will try to give a suitable answer.
To Japan, I aim to contribute actively to its physics community by engaging in cutting-edge physics research. As a dedicated student, I will bring my mathematical rigor and interdisciplinary perspective—shaped by my studies in mathematics and oceanography—to collaborative research projects. My thesis was about detecting turbulence and tracking them, and working in such field gave me many skills. One of my experience is in computational physics will be useful in all branches of physics. Working in such field gave me a perception in non-linear system and practical background in partial differential equation. These knowledge could offer fresh insights into physics research.
To Iran, I can share advanced knowledge after my return through teaching, workshops or research collaborations. For example, I can develop educational resources inspired by innovative Japanese teaching methods and make complex physics concepts more accessible to Iranian students. Because the Iranian physics community faces challenges such as limited access to some laboratories and observatories, and since this access is one of the important factors in creating a new theory, I will try to set up such facilities in Iran. For example, Iran has one of the oldest observatories in the world, but it is no longer a leader in this field. Certainly, seeing the advanced facilities in Japan will allow me to play a positive role in promoting these facilities.
